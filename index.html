San Francisco
After Dark

A decadal analysis exploring the shifting patterns of urban crime and the digital shadows of public safety.

Published

April 6, 2026

Authors

Nick Tahmasebi, Filip Sahakian, Destin Zhong

T he last ten years have seen an unprecedented shift in how we observe, report, and analyze safety in our major metropolitan centers. As cities grow smarter, the data they generate becomes the primary lens through which we view social progress.

Behind the headlines of fluctuating statistics lies a complex interplay of socioeconomic factors, surveillance integration, and community-led initiatives. This report synthesizes over 12 million data points sourced from open-source law enforcement repositories to paint a precise picture of our urban reality.

We move beyond the raw numbers to look at the "pulse" of the city—mapping how incidents cluster, fade, and migrate across district lines over the span of a decade.

The decentralization of crime is perhaps the most significant finding of our decadal study. Traditional "high-activity zones" are no longer as static as they were in 2014. Predictive policing models and economic shifts have pushed these patterns outward, creating a more diffused safety landscape.

"A 15% reduction in property crime over five years marks a pivotal shift in metropolitan stability."

— Data Source: Municipal Archive Alpha

However, this reduction in traditional property crime is mirrored by a rise in digital and non-physical incidents, a transition that many departments are still struggling to quantify accurately within existing frameworks.
